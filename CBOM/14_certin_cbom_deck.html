CERT-In's CBOM guidelines establish India's national standard for cryptographic component visibility in software systems. For organizations operating in regulated sectors — banking, healthcare, government, and critical infrastructure — compliance is not optional. This episode breaks down exactly what is required.

⚖️ Why the Guidelines Were Issued
  • 🔍
    No crypto visibility — most Indian organizations had zero visibility into which cryptographic components their software used, making risk assessment impossible.
  • 🌐
    Global supply chain risk — Log4Shell and similar incidents showed that unknown transitive dependencies create massive, undetected attack surfaces.
  • ⚛️
    Quantum computing preparedness — India's national security posture requires mapping all current crypto to plan migration to post-quantum algorithms (PQC) before quantum-capable adversaries emerge.
  • 📋
    Audit and accountability gap — regulators needed a standardized format to verify cryptographic compliance during audits — without a standard, every organization reported differently.

⚖️ Legal Weight: Non-compliance with CERT-In directives can result in regulatory action under the IT Act 2000. For critical infrastructure operators, failure to maintain a CBOM is increasingly treated as a material security control gap during government and RBI audits.

📋 Core Requirements · Slide 3 of 6

Key CBOM Requirements under CERT-In

📖 CERT-In's CBOM mandate establishes four core requirement pillars. Each pillar defines a specific obligation — what must be tracked, how it must be structured, and what governance processes must be in place. Together they create a complete cryptographic governance framework for any organization handling sensitive data.

📦

Maintain Complete Crypto Inventory

Mandatory
✅
All cryptographic algorithms actively used in production systems must be documented — including indirect dependencies introduced by third-party libraries.
✅
Inventory must cover all environments: development, staging, production, and disaster recovery — not just production alone.
✅
Both intentional and transitive (indirect) crypto components must be included — no component may be excluded due to being automatically introduced.
🔐

Cover All Crypto Component Types

Mandatory
⚙️
Algorithms — type, name, mode, and strength classification (strong / deprecated / broken). Deprecated algorithms must be flagged and remediation planned.
🗝️
Keys — type (symmetric/asymmetric), bit length, storage mechanism, rotation policy, and last-rotation date must all be recorded.
📜
Certificates — issuer, subject, expiry, signature algorithm, and revocation status tracked for every TLS/SSL certificate in use.
🔗
Protocols — TLS version, SSH version, and cipher suite configuration must be documented and compared against approved minimum standards.
✔️

Ensure Accuracy & Completeness

Required
🎯
CBOM data must reflect the actual deployed state — not last month's snapshot. Stale CBOMs that don't reflect current deployments are non-compliant.
🔏
CBOM documents must be digitally signed by the generating tool or authorized security officer to prove provenance and prevent tampering.
📅
Each CBOM entry must carry a verified generation timestamp. For compliance audits, regulators may ask for CBOMs at specific points in time.
👁️

Maintain Visibility & Reporting

Required
📊
CBOM must be accessible to security and compliance teams through a centralized platform — not distributed across individual developer machines.
🚨
Organizations must have alerting in place for: deprecated algorithm usage, certificate expiry within 30 days, and new CVEs affecting CBOM components.
📤
On request from CERT-In or designated regulatory bodies, the CBOM must be shareable in standard machine-readable format (CycloneDX JSON or SPDX) within a defined SLA.

📌 CERT-In Alignment Note: These requirements align with the broader CERT-In Information Security Practices directive and the NCIIPC (National Critical Information Infrastructure Protection Centre) guidelines for protecting India's critical digital infrastructure through comprehensive asset visibility.

⚙️ Implementation · Slide 5 of 6

Implementation & Operational Expectations

📖 CERT-In does not consider CBOM a one-time deliverable — it is an ongoing operational discipline. The guidelines specify expectations around frequency of updates, tool integration, continuous monitoring, and governance processes that organizations must embed into their standard security operations.

🔄

Regular CBOM Updates

1
Regenerate CBOM on every code commit, build, and deployment — not on a monthly or quarterly schedule
2
Trigger re-scan when any dependency changes — even indirect package-lock updates must invalidate and regenerate the CBOM
3
Maintain a versioned CBOM archive — auditors may request the CBOM as it existed on a specific past date
🔌

Security Tool Integration

1
CBOM must feed into SIEM, vulnerability management, and CSPM platforms for automated risk correlation
2
Integrate with CVE databases (NVD, OSS Index) for automatic vulnerability matching against every CBOM component version
3
Embed in CI/CD pipeline with policy-as-code gates — weak crypto in CBOM must fail the build before deployment
📡

Continuous Monitoring

1
Runtime environment scanning must supplement static CBOM — Kubernetes pods, cloud services, and containers scanned continuously
2
Certificate expiry monitoring with automated alerts at 90, 30, and 7-day thresholds — escalating to security leadership at 7 days
3
New CVE publications must trigger automatic re-evaluation of all CBOM entries — within 24 hours for Critical severity
⚠️

Risk Management

1
Deprecated algorithms flagged in CBOM must have a documented remediation timeline — not just flagged and ignored
2
Risk acceptance decisions (where immediate upgrade is not feasible) must be documented, approved by CISO, and tracked in the CBOM system
3
CBOM risk posture must be reported to senior management quarterly as part of the security governance review cadence
📊

Governance & Reporting

1
Assign a CBOM Owner — a named security role responsible for CBOM accuracy, completeness, and regulatory reporting
2
Maintain an approved-algorithm policy document aligned with CERT-In, NIST, and FIPS 140-3 — reviewed and updated annually
3
Be prepared to submit the current CBOM to CERT-In within the defined incident-response window if a cryptographic breach is suspected
🗂️

Format & Standards

1
CBOM must be produced in CycloneDX JSON 1.5+ or SPDX format — proprietary or unstructured formats are not compliant
2
Documents must carry a digital signature (JWS) from the generating tool or authorized security officer to prove authenticity
3
Store CBOMs in a tamper-evident repository (immutable object storage, signed artefact registry) with access-controlled retrieval

Summary & Key Takeaways

🏛️

CERT-In = Legal Foundation

India's national cybersecurity authority mandates CBOM for critical infrastructure operators. Non-compliance is a regulatory risk, not just a best-practice gap.

📋

Mandatory Data Fields

Component identification, cryptographic details, certificate metadata, and usage/dependency information are all required — incomplete records are non-compliant.

🔄

Continuous — Not One-Time

CBOM must be regenerated on every build, integrated into CI/CD, linked to CVE databases, and monitored continuously — it is an operational discipline, not an annual audit deliverable.

🎯 The Core Message

CBOM is not just a compliance checkbox — it is the foundation of modern cryptographic governance. Organisations that implement CERT-In-compliant CBOMs gain complete visibility into their cryptographic posture, dramatically reduce their attack surface, and position themselves for quantum-era security migration. In today's DevSecOps landscape, you cannot govern what you cannot see — and CBOM makes everything visible.
