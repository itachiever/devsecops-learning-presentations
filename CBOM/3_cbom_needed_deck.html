Problem: Lack of Cryptographic Visibility

🔍

"No Visibility = High Risk" — You cannot protect what you cannot see. Most teams have zero awareness of which cryptographic libraries are running inside their applications.

🚫 What Organizations Don't Know
  • 🔐
    What encryption is being used — third-party libraries, open-source packages, and legacy code often use unknown algorithms without documentation.
  • 📍
    Where encryption is used — encryption may exist in payment modules, auth systems, or APIs, but there is no central map of where or how.
  • 📦
    Which crypto libraries are included — a single npm or Maven package can pull in 5–10 indirect cryptographic dependencies silently.
  • 🔄
    Who updates them — if you don't know a library exists, it never gets patched when a vulnerability is discovered.
📌 Real-World Example
🏗️

The Hidden Dependency Problem

A DevOps team deploys a Node.js app. Buried inside a transitive dependency, an old version of OpenSSL with a known flaw is running. The team never audited it — because they didn't know it existed.

📊

The Scale of the Problem

  • A typical enterprise app has 50–200+ indirect dependencies
  • Only ~30% of crypto components are intentionally chosen
  • The rest arrive silently through package managers

💡 Key Insight: You can't fix, audit, or govern cryptographic components that you don't know exist. The first step to security is always complete visibility.

❌ Problem 2 of 3 · Slide 3 of 6

Problem: Weak & Outdated Cryptography

⚠️

Not all encryption is equal. Many applications still rely on algorithms that were broken years ago. Hackers actively exploit these weaknesses — and most teams don't even know these algorithms are in use.

💀

MD5 — Completely Broken

Designed in 1991. Researchers proved in 2004 it can be reversed and forged. Still found in legacy codebases for password hashing, checksums, and tokens.

❌ Do not use for any security purpose

⚡

SHA-1 — Practically Broken

Google's SHAttered attack (2017) created two different files with the same SHA-1 hash in under 100,000 CPU hours. Still used in legacy TLS certificates and git repositories.

⚠️ Deprecated — migrate to SHA-256/SHA-3

🔌

Old TLS Versions (1.0 / 1.1)

TLS 1.0 (1999) and TLS 1.1 (2006) have known vulnerabilities like POODLE and BEAST attacks. Both are officially deprecated by IETF since 2021 but still active on legacy servers.

⚠️ Upgrade to TLS 1.2 minimum, TLS 1.3 preferred

💥

Data Breaches

Attackers crack weak hashes from stolen databases and gain access to user accounts en masse.

🎭

Man-in-the-Middle Attacks

Old TLS lets attackers silently intercept and read traffic between users and servers.

⚛️

Quantum Threat Ahead

Quantum computers will break RSA-2048 and ECC. Algorithms need mapping now to plan migration.

❌ Problem 3 of 3 · Slide 4 of 6

Problem: Certificate & TLS Configuration Issues

📜 What Can Go Wrong with Certificates?
  • 📅
    Expired certificates — SSL/TLS certificates have expiry dates. When they expire, browsers show a "Not Secure" or "Your connection is not private" warning, breaking user trust instantly.
  • ⚙️
    Misconfigured TLS — wrong cipher suites, weak key lengths (RSA-1024), or missing HSTS headers leave connections vulnerable even when a cert is valid.
  • 🏷️
    Wrong domain or scope — a certificate issued for api.example.com used on app.example.com throws browser errors and blocks legitimate users.
  • 🔗
    Broken certificate chain — intermediate CA certificates not properly installed cause verification failures on some devices and clients.
  • 🏛️
    Untrusted Certificate Authority — self-signed or expired root CAs trigger hard browser blocks, causing operational outages in production.

⚠️ Operational Risk: A single expired certificate can take down an entire production service — payment APIs, login endpoints, or public websites — affecting thousands of users within seconds.

✅ Solution · Slide 5 of 6

How CBOM Solves These Problems

🛡️ A CBOM (Cryptographic Bill of Materials) is a centralized, structured inventory of every cryptographic component in your software — algorithms, keys, certificates, protocols — giving you complete control & governance over your security posture.

👁️

Full Crypto Visibility

Automatically discovers every crypto component — even in third-party and transitive dependencies. Nothing is hidden.

🔍

Weak Crypto Detection

Flags deprecated algorithms (MD5, SHA-1, DES) and old protocols (TLS 1.0/1.1) before attackers can exploit them.

📜

Certificate Tracking

Monitors all certificates — expiry dates, issuer, strength — and alerts teams weeks before expiration causes outages.

📋

Compliance Ready

Proves to auditors (FIPS, PCI-DSS, HIPAA, NIS2) exactly which approved algorithms and protocols are in use.

⚛️

Quantum Readiness

Maps current algorithms so teams can plan ahead for post-quantum cryptography (PQC) migration before it's urgent.

🔄

Continuous Monitoring

Integrates into CI/CD pipelines — scans every code change and deployment so crypto risks are caught in real time.

🛠️ Tools that generate CBOMs: Syft · cdxgen · Dependabot (crypto plugin) · OWASP CycloneDX · IBM SBOM Utility

🎯 End result: Turn invisible cryptographic risk into a managed, trackable, governable inventory — one that your security, DevOps, and compliance teams all share.
