🌐 Foundation — Slide 2 of 6

Why Do Applications Need Security?

⚠️ Without Encryption — Real Danger
  • 🏦
    Banking: Without encryption, your account number & password travel as plain readable text — anyone on the network can steal it.
  • 🔐
    Login Pages: Your username & password could be captured by hackers using simple "packet sniffing" tools.
  • 📧
    Emails: Without encryption, every email you send can be read by your Internet Service Provider or anyone in between.
  • 🛒
    Online Shopping: Credit card details would be visible in plain text — identity theft in seconds.
✅ With Encryption — You Are Protected
🔒 HTTPS

Secure Web Browsing

When you see https:// in your browser, it means all data between you and the website is encrypted. A hacker can see you visited the site, but not what you typed.

🗝️ Passwords

Password Hashing

Websites never store your actual password. They store a hash — a scrambled fingerprint. Even if hackers steal the database, they can't reverse-engineer your original password easily.

💡 Simple Analogy: Encryption is like writing a letter in a secret language only the intended reader knows. Even if someone intercepts the envelope, they just see gibberish.

🔬 Core Concept — Slide 3 of 6

What is Cryptography?

📖 Cryptography is the science of protecting information by transforming it into an unreadable form — so only authorized people can read it. It's the backbone of all digital security.

🔄

Encryption

Converts readable data (plaintext) into scrambled data (ciphertext) using a key. Only someone with the correct key can decrypt it back.

  • AES-256 — used in HTTPS, VPNs
  • RSA — used in digital certificates
  • ChaCha20 — used in modern TLS
🧮

Hashing

A one-way function — converts data into a fixed-size fingerprint. You can't reverse it. Used to verify integrity, not for secrecy.

  • SHA-256 — blockchain, certificates
  • bcrypt / Argon2 — password storage
  • ❌ MD5 / SHA-1 — outdated, weak!
🌍

Real-World Usage

  • 🌐 HTTPS websites use TLS (encryption)
  • 🔑 SSH login uses RSA/Ed25519 keys
  • 🛡️ WhatsApp uses E2E encryption
  • 💳 Banking uses AES + RSA
  • 🖊️ Code signing uses digital certs

💡 Think of it this way: Encryption = a lockbox you can open. Hashing = a fingerprint scanner — unique per input, impossible to reverse. Both protect, but in different ways.

📋 The Main Topic — Slide 4 of 6

What is CBOM?

📖 A Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM) is a structured inventory of all cryptographic components used inside a software application or system — algorithms, keys, certificates, and protocols — all in one place.

🗂️ What Does a CBOM Track?
⚙️

Algorithms

Which encryption methods are in use — e.g., AES-256, RSA-2048, SHA-256. Weak algorithms (MD5, DES) are flagged.

🗝️

Keys

Tracks encryption keys — their type, length, and where they are used. Short or weak keys are a risk.

📜

Certificates

SSL/TLS certificates used by services — who issued them, when they expire, and if they're still valid.

🔗

Protocols

Which security protocols are in use — e.g., TLS 1.2, TLS 1.3, SSH. Old protocols like SSLv3 are dangerous.

🍔 Real-World Analogy

💡 Just like a food product's ingredient label tells you exactly what's inside (fats, sugar, additives), a CBOM tells you exactly what crypto is inside your software — so you know if it's safe to "consume".

🏗️

Who Creates a CBOM?

  • Security teams during code audits
  • DevSecOps engineers in CI/CD pipelines
  • Automated scanning tools (e.g., Syft, cdxgen)
  • Software vendors for compliance
🚨 Why It Matters — Slide 5 of 6

Why is CBOM Important?

❌ Problems Without CBOM
  • 🔓 Weak algorithms still in use — MD5 & SHA-1 are broken, yet many apps still use them unknowingly.
  • 📅 Expired TLS certificates — cause outages, trust failures, and security gaps. Often discovered too late.
  • 🕵️ Unknown crypto usage — developers don't know which crypto libraries third-party code is using.
  • ⚠️ Quantum risk exposure — many current algorithms will be broken by quantum computers. You can't migrate what you haven't mapped.
  • 🚫 Compliance failures — regulations (FIPS, PCI-DSS, HIPAA) require approved algorithms. Without CBOM, audits fail.
✅ How CBOM Helps
  • 👁️ Full Visibility — Know every single cryptographic component used — no hidden surprises. Complete audit trail.
  • 🔧 Risk Reduction — Identify and replace weak/outdated crypto before attackers exploit it. Proactive security.
  • ⏰ Certificate Monitoring — Get alerts before certificates expire. Prevent unexpected service outages.
  • ⚛️ Quantum Readiness — Map current crypto to plan migration to post-quantum algorithms (PQC) in advance.
  • ✅ Compliance Ready — Prove to auditors exactly which approved algorithms are in use. Faster, cleaner audits.

🎯 Bottom Line: You can't fix what you don't know. CBOM gives security teams complete visibility into their cryptographic landscape — turning unknown risks into manageable, trackable items.
